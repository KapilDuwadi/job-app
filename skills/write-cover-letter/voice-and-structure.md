# Cover letter — voice & structure

## Structure (3–4 short paragraphs)

1. **Hook (2–3 sentences).** Name the role and the company, and give one *specific, genuine* reason
   you're writing to them: a product you admire, a problem their team is solving that matches your
   experience, a mission you connect with. Never "I am excited to apply for this opportunity."

2. **Evidence (3–5 sentences).** Your one or two strongest, most relevant accomplishments — the ones
   that map directly to the posting's top must-haves (see `analysis.md`). Show outcomes with real
   numbers. This is the paragraph that earns the interview; make it concrete, not adjectival.

3. **Fit (2–4 sentences).** Connect your trajectory to where the team/company is going, and what
   you'd contribute in the first few months. Two-way match, not just "I want this job".

4. **Close (1–2 sentences).** Confident, warm, brief. Thank them and invite a conversation. No
   groveling, no pressure.

## Voice
- **Match the user.** If `writing-samples/` exist, mirror their sentence length, warmth, and
  vocabulary. Otherwise: professional, direct, human — how a thoughtful person actually writes.
- Match the **posting's tone** too: a mission-driven startup letter reads differently from a bank's.

## Anti-clichés — cut these on sight
- "I am excited to apply / thrilled at the opportunity"
- "I am a hard worker / team player / fast learner" (show, don't assert)
- "I believe I would be a great fit" (demonstrate the fit instead)
- "Please find attached my resume"
- "Dear Sir or Madam" (use "Dear Hiring Manager" if no name)
- Restating the whole resume in prose — the letter adds context and voice, it doesn't duplicate.

## Mechanics
- One page, comfortably. Shorter and sharper beats long.
- Address a real name if the posting/company page reveals one; else "Dear Hiring Manager,".
- Every claim must be true and traceable to the master resume.
- Escape LaTeX specials (`& % $ # _ { } ~ ^`), `---` for em-dashes.
